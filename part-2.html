You've been watching the wrong revolution.

For three years, every conference keynote, every investor memo, every breathless LinkedIn post has fixated on the same thing: artificial intelligence is transforming knowledge work. Software engineers, lawyers, analysts, writers — their jobs are changing, maybe disappearing, definitely getting weird. The discourse has been relentless and, frankly, exhausting.

Meanwhile, something quieter and far more consequential has been happening in the physical world. Or rather, not happening. America is trying to rebuild its industrial base — a bipartisan consensus so rare it might be the last thing both parties agree on before the heat death of the universe — and it is failing. Not for lack of money. Not for lack of political will. For lack of people who can program CNC machines.

This is not a metaphor. This is not an abstraction about "the future of work." There are 354,800 machinists in the United States right now (Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2024 Occupational Outlook Handbook). The largest age cohort is 55 to 59 years old. The BLS projects that machinists as an occupation will decline by 2% over the next decade. Every single one of the 34,200 annual job openings is a replacement opening — someone retiring, someone leaving. Not a single one represents growth.

Read that again. The occupation that makes every physical thing in the advanced economy — every aircraft component, every surgical instrument, every defense system, every piece of the infrastructure we're betting $602 billion on this year — is shrinking.

And we're acting like the big story is whether ChatGPT can write a legal brief.

The Industrial Base Cannot Surge

There is a concept in defense planning called "surge capacity" — the ability to rapidly scale production when a crisis demands it. It is the foundation on which deterrence rests. If your adversaries believe you can outproduce them when it matters, they think twice. If they know you can't, they don't.

America's surge capacity is broken.

The most vivid proof is sitting in ammunition depots across the country. In early 2022, when Russia invaded Ukraine, the United States was producing approximately 14,000 155mm artillery shells per month. These are not exotic weapons. They are the bread and butter of conventional warfare — steel tubes filled with explosives, a design that has been fundamentally unchanged for decades. The kind of thing an industrial superpower should be able to crank out.

The Pentagon set a target: 100,000 rounds per month. Three years later, production sits at 40,000. The Army doesn't expect to hit 100,000 until mid-2026 — and that timeline has already slipped multiple times. The Center for Strategic and International Studies noted that the Army's struggles are "linked to the state of the industrial base before the Russian invasion of Ukraine."

We could not triple production of a simple munition in three years.

Now consider what isn't simple. The F-35 Lightning II, the most advanced fighter aircraft ever built, had over 4,000 parts shortages on Lockheed Martin's assembly line as of early 2025 — double historic levels. Fifty-two aircraft sat stalled in final assembly, waiting for components. Average delivery delays hit 238 days.

The Heritage Foundation's assessment uses language you don't normally hear from a think tank known for measured analysis. The domestic defense industrial base has "slowly declined as domestic defense production has consolidated and American manufacturing has moved overseas." Decades of poor policymaking have led to "underinvestment, regulatory drag, labor misalignment and market distortions." All of this in what the Heritage Foundation calls "the most hostile global environment since World War II."

The National Defense University Press put it more bluntly: "A growing chorus of U.S. defense analysts, lawmakers, and military officials has emphasized that the United States lacks the munitions production capacity to meet the demands of the contemporary strategic environment."

This is the backdrop against which the reshoring consensus is supposed to deliver results. Bipartisan agreement that America needs to make things again. Hundreds of billions in subsidies and incentives — CHIPS Act, IRA, defense appropriations. Political consensus, capital, demand.

But no one to do the work.

The Workforce That Isn't Coming

Let me give you the full picture of the machinist workforce, because the aggregate statistics don't capture how dire this actually is.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics counts 354,800 machinists and tool-and-die makers in the United States as of 2024. The median wage is $56,150 per year — decent, but not the kind of money that draws ambitious young people away from tech or finance. The three largest age cohorts are 55–59 (47,710 workers), 60–64 (43,797), and 50–54 (43,138), according to American Community Survey data compiled by Data USA. That means roughly 134,645 machinists — 39% of the entire workforce — are in the 50+ age brackets. They are, on actuarial tables if not in spirit, heading for the exits.

The pipeline to replace them is, to use a technical term, nonexistent. The BLS projects a decline of 5,900 machinists over the next decade. The 34,200 annual openings are all replacement — and the declining total tells you those openings aren't all being filled. There is no authoritative national count of how many CNC machinists graduate from training programs each year, which tells you something about how seriously we've been tracking this.

Zoom out from machinists to all of manufacturing and the picture gets worse. Deloitte and the Manufacturing Institute projected in 2021 that 2.1 million manufacturing jobs could go unfilled by 2030, at a cost to the US economy of up to $1 trillion. That number was calculated before the reshoring push, before the defense buildup, before $602 billion in datacenter capex. The actual gap is almost certainly larger.

Here's the part that makes this a structural problem rather than a cyclical one: wages aren't rising fast enough to close it. Machinist wages have been relatively flat in real terms. In a functioning labor market, acute shortages drive wages up, which draws new workers in. That isn't happening because the shops can't afford to pay more — their margins are already thin, their customers won't absorb higher prices, and they're competing against overseas production where labor costs a fraction of what it does here. It's a death spiral: low wages → fewer entrants → deeper shortage → still can't raise wages because the economics don't work → more people leave for easier careers.

Every factory owner I've ever spoken to — and I've spoken to hundreds — gives the same answer when asked about their biggest problem. It's not materials costs. It's not machine availability. It's not demand. It's finding people who can program CNC machines.

Every. Single. One.

What Lockheed Martin Already Knows

A senior Lockheed Martin Ventures executive told me: "They could not hire all the machinists in the world and be able to make what they're being asked to make by the Pentagon."

Sit with that for a moment. This isn't a pundit speculating. This isn't a politician grandstanding. This is someone inside the largest defense contractor on Earth — a company with $67 billion in annual revenue and access to every lever of American industrial power — telling you that hiring your way out of this problem is mathematically impossible. Even if you could hire every machinist alive, the capacity wouldn't exist to meet what the Pentagon needs.

The bottleneck is not machines. America has machines. The bottleneck is not money. The defense budget is at historic highs. The bottleneck is the human capacity to program those machines — to take a CAD model of a component and turn it into the sequence of instructions that tells a CNC machine how to cut metal, at what speed, with which tools, in what order, without crashing, without wasting material, without exceeding tolerances.

This is called CAM programming — Computer-Aided Manufacturing — and it is one of the most difficult and underappreciated skills in the modern economy. There are more possible ways to machine a typical component than there are atoms in the universe, multiplied by roughly ten billion. I'm not being hyperbolic. The combinatorial space of tool selection, cutting strategies, sequencing, fixturing, speeds, feeds, and collision avoidance is genuinely astronomical. It takes years of training and experience to get good at it. And the people who are good at it are retiring.

The Reshoring Math Doesn't Work — Until It Does

Everyone agrees America should make more things domestically. The reasons are obvious: supply chain resilience, national security, job creation, reduced dependence on geopolitical adversaries. The problem is that reshoring, as currently conceived, is a fantasy.

Here's why. The reason manufacturing moved overseas in the first place wasn't because Chinese or Vietnamese factory workers are better. It's because they're cheaper. Dramatically cheaper. The labor cost per part in Shenzhen versus Indianapolis is so large that companies will happily pay to ship components across an ocean, clear customs, manage longer lead times, and accept the quality risks of distant supply chains. The shipping costs, the logistics headaches, the inventory buffers — all of it is still cheaper than American labor.

Subsidies can close part of that gap, but they're temporary and politically fragile. Tariffs can close part of it, but they raise costs for everyone downstream and invite retaliation. Neither addresses the fundamental problem: even if you wanted to make things in America, you don't have enough people who know how.

But there's a version of this math that does work, and it depends on a variable most reshoring advocates haven't thought about carefully enough: the cost of the person in the part.

When a CNC programmer spends eight hours writing a program for a component, the cost of that labor gets embedded in every part that program produces. If the programmer can produce one or two programs per day — which is typical for complex parts — then human programming time is a significant fraction of the per-unit cost. And it's the fraction where the US-to-overseas delta is widest.

Now imagine that same programmer produces ten to twenty programs per day. Not because they're cutting corners or skipping quality checks, but because AI handles the repetitive, time-intensive portions of CAM programming — the roughing strategies, the standard feature recognition, the tool selection for common operations — while the human focuses on the hard parts: process planning, tolerance management, the stuff that requires judgment and experience.

When that happens, the labor cost per part drops dramatically. And when the labor cost drops, the delta between domestic and overseas production shrinks. Suddenly, the cost of putting things on boats — shipping, customs, inventory carrying costs, lead time risk — becomes the dominant factor. The economics of proximity start to win.

One person becomes what I've started calling a one-person machining army. They're not doing less-skilled work. They're doing more of the highest-skilled work — the judgment calls, the process optimization, the problem-solving that machines can't do — while AI handles the drudgery that currently consumes most of their day.

That's not a theory. It's happening right now, in hundreds of machine shops around the world.

The Drudgery Problem

I need you to understand what CNC programming actually looks like in practice, because the gap between what outsiders imagine and what actually happens is vast.

Most people, if they think about CNC machining at all, picture a skilled craftsperson at a gleaming machine, carefully shaping metal into precision components. The reality is that most of a CNC programmer's time is spent fighting software. CAM software — the programs used to generate toolpaths — is legendarily bad. The interfaces are arcane, the workflows are convoluted, and what should be straightforward tasks become exercises in digital bureaucracy.

It can take five to fifteen minutes just to get a part oriented correctly in the virtual vise inside a CAM package. Not because the programmer doesn't know what they're doing, but because the software is so unwieldy that basic operations require clicking through nested menus and configuring parameters that should be defaults. Multiply that by every operation on every feature of every part, and a programmer who could be thinking about process optimization — the creative, intellectually demanding work — is instead doing the digital equivalent of filling out forms.

The industry average for spindle uptime — the percentage of time a CNC machine is actually cutting metal, which is the only time it makes money — is less than 50% during operating hours. Not 50% of a 24-hour day. Fifty percent of the shift when someone is supposedly running it. The other half is setup, programming, waiting for programs, troubleshooting. And most shops can't run additional shifts because they don't have enough programmers.

This is a staggering amount of latent capacity sitting idle inside existing machine shops. You don't need to build new factories. You need to unlock the factories you already have.

Lead times in the industry are typically six to twelve weeks. Customers want to order more but can't because shops don't have the programming capacity to take on additional work. It's not a demand problem. It's a supply problem, and the constraint is human attention, not machine availability.

The tools we've been given are not nearly good enough. That's the honest truth. People in machining end up doing boring, repetitive drudgery work because the software is so weak. They deserve better. And when they get better tools, they don't become less valuable — they become more valuable, because they can finally spend their time on the work that actually requires their expertise.

AI That Understands Metal

This is where the AI revolution intersects with the physical world in a way that most observers have completely missed.

There's a widespread assumption that artificial intelligence is primarily a knowledge-work technology — that it automates text, code, and images, not physical processes. This is wrong in an important way. AI doesn't need to physically operate a machine to transform manufacturing. It needs to automate the cognitive bottleneck: the programming step that sits between a design and a finished part.

But — and this is critical — you can't just point a large language model at a CAD file and ask it to generate a toolpath. An LLM has no understanding of cutting forces, tool deflection, chip evacuation, workholding constraints, or any of the thousand physical realities that determine whether a machining program produces a good part or destroys a $50,000 spindle. The primitives don't exist in its training data. Machining is not text.

What you need is purpose-built AI infrastructure: machining strategy engines, tool libraries, collision detection systems, mixed-integer programming solvers that can navigate the combinatorial explosion of possible manufacturing approaches. You need systems that understand the physics and the practice of cutting metal, built by people who have actually done it.

This is a ten-year problem, not a weekend hackathon. And it's why the venture capital market is paying attention.

The Money Sees Something

In the last twelve months, three manufacturing AI companies have raised a combined $510 million in venture capital.

Hadrian raised a $260 million Series C in July 2025, led by Founders Fund and Lux Capital, with participation from a16z, Morgan Stanley, and others. Their thesis: AI-powered factories for precision machining, focused on defense and space. They're building new physical factories from scratch — capex-heavy, hardware-plus-software, "factories-as-a-service."

Bright Machines raised $126 million in May 2025, led by BlackRock, with NVIDIA and Microsoft participating. Software-defined manufacturing focused on robotic assembly.

Machina Labs raised $124 million in March 2026 for AI-driven metal forming, backed by the US Air Force.

This is the hottest the manufacturing AI space has ever been. The investors behind these rounds include some of the most sophisticated firms in technology: Founders Fund, a16z, Lux Capital, BlackRock, NVIDIA, Microsoft. These are not people who throw money at problems that don't exist.

They see what the BLS data shows: that America's machining capacity is structurally declining at the exact moment when demand is structurally increasing. They see that the gap cannot be closed by training more humans, because the training pipeline is broken and the demographic math doesn't work. And they see that AI, applied correctly to the right bottleneck, is the only lever that scales fast enough.

There are two basic approaches to this problem. One is to build entirely new automated factories — Hadrian's approach. This requires enormous capital, years of construction, and the development of novel hardware systems. It's a valid strategy, but it's slow and expensive.

The other approach is to take the existing installed base of CNC machines — hundreds of thousands of them, sitting in shops across America and the world — and make the people operating them dramatically more productive. Not by replacing them. By giving them AI tools that handle the repetitive, time-consuming portions of their work, so they can do ten times as much of the high-value work that only they can do.

The first approach builds new capacity. The second unlocks capacity that already exists. Both are necessary. But only the second can deliver results at the speed the current crisis demands, because the machines are already there. The shops are already there. The machinists are already there — for now.

What 10x Productivity Actually Looks Like

I want to be specific, because "AI makes people more productive" is the kind of vague claim that makes machinists — rightfully — roll their eyes.

A machine shop in the Netherlands called Frerotech adopted AI-powered CAM software and documented a 60 to 95 percent reduction in programming time. The evaluation wasn't done by a sales team or a marketing department. It was conducted by a university intern who independently assessed multiple competing solutions before recommending one. When someone who has no financial incentive to exaggerate tells you they're saving 60 to 95 percent of their programming time, you should pay attention.

Arcamed, a shop in Indianapolis that manufactures surgical trays, reported 75% process completion rates with AI-assisted CAM programming, noting that it was "decreasing overhead cost which directly affects customer costs." This isn't a tech demo. It's a medical manufacturing company telling you their per-part costs are going down because programming takes less time.

A UK shop called Bedford CNC described the experience as "100% different from anything I've used before."

These are real shops making real parts for real customers. They're not replacing machinists. They're giving machinists superpowers — the ability to do in hours what used to take days, the ability to take on work they previously had to turn away, the ability to run shifts they previously couldn't staff.

The most valuable workers at these shops — the ones who become irreplaceable — are invariably the heaviest adopters of AI tools. This is counterintuitive if you believe AI replaces human skill, but obvious if you understand what's actually happening. The most skilled machinists know what questions to ask. They know what a good process looks like. They have judgment that no AI can replicate. When you give them tools that handle the mechanical parts of programming, they don't become less skilled — they become more dangerous. They can apply their hard-won expertise to ten times as many parts.

For the first time ever, a machinist can scale their own intelligence. Their experience, their intuition, their accumulated knowledge of how metal behaves — all of it gets amplified by systems that can capture and replicate the patterns, freeing the human to focus on the exceptions, the novel problems, the things that actually require a brain.

This is not a threat to machinists. It is the greatest opportunity the profession has seen in a generation.

The $602 Billion Tailwind

If you think the machining capacity crisis is just a defense problem, you haven't been paying attention to what the technology industry is building.

In 2026, the five largest hyperscale cloud companies — Amazon, Google, Microsoft, Meta, and Oracle — are projected to spend a combined $602 billion in capital expenditure. Roughly 75% of that, approximately $450 billion, is going directly to AI infrastructure. This represents a 36% increase over 2025 and has pushed capital intensity to 45–57% of revenue — historically unthinkable levels for companies this large.

That money is not just buying GPUs. It's building physical infrastructure: datacenter buildings, power distribution systems, and — critically — cooling systems. As GPU power density increases (NVIDIA's GB200 draws over 1,000 watts per chip), air cooling becomes physically inadequate. The industry is in the middle of a forced transition to direct liquid cooling, and every liquid-cooled GPU rack requires precision-machined cold plates, manifolds, and fittings.

The liquid cooling market for datacenters is projected to grow from roughly $3–5 billion in 2025 to $15–27 billion by the early 2030s. That market is almost entirely dependent on CNC-machined components — copper and aluminum cold plates with micro-channel structures that must be machined to tight tolerances.

Beyond cooling, datacenters need machined rack hardware, power distribution busbars, cable management systems, and structural components. Many hyperscalers are building dedicated power generation facilities — gas turbines, and eventually small modular nuclear reactors — all of which require precision machined parts: turbine components, valve bodies, pump housings, flanges.

Conservative estimates suggest $30–100 million in machined components per $1 billion of datacenter construction, heavily dependent on cooling architecture. If even 2–5% of the $602 billion in 2026 hyperscaler capex flows to machining, that's $12–30 billion in annual demand from datacenters alone — on top of existing defense, aerospace, automotive, medical, and industrial demand.

This is happening in an economy with 354,800 machinists, a number that is declining.

The Window

I started my company ten years ago because I believed CNC machining was an impossibly hard problem worth spending a career on. There are more ways to machine a typical component than there are atoms in the universe, by an additional factor of about ten billion. I'm not being hyperbolic. The combinatorial space of tool selection, cutting strategies, sequencing, fixturing, speeds, feeds, and collision avoidance is genuinely astronomical. It takes years of training and experience to get good at it. And the people who are good at it are retiring.

But impossible problems are only impossible until they're not. Over the past decade, the AI infrastructure required to tackle manufacturing programming has matured — not the flashy, headline-grabbing AI, but the deep technical substrate: machining strategy engines, physics-aware optimization, tool library management, collision detection. The foundational tools that an LLM needs to reason over before it can be useful in a machine shop.

The venture market has noticed. Strategic defense and technology investors have noticed. The government has noticed. $108.5 million in funding from investors including Autodesk — the world's largest CAD/CAM company — Lockheed Martin, and In-Q-Tel, the CIA's strategic venture arm, suggests that the people closest to the problem believe AI-powered CAM is not just viable but essential.

But here's what keeps me up at night: the window is closing.

The 47,710 machinists in the 55–59 age bracket are five to ten years from retirement. The 43,797 in the 60–64 bracket are leaving now. Every one of them takes decades of accumulated knowledge — the tribal knowledge of how to hold a difficult part, the intuition for when a tool is about to fail, the sixth sense for a process that's drifting out of tolerance — and that knowledge walks out the door with them. No training manual captures it. No corporate knowledge base preserves it.

AI can capture some of it. AI-powered CAM systems that learn from thousands of successful machining operations can encode patterns that would otherwise vanish with each retirement. But this only works if the technology is deployed while the experienced machinists are still there to teach it. Once they're gone, the knowledge is gone. We're not just racing against a labor shortage. We're racing against an information loss event.

The Deloitte projection of 2.1 million unfilled manufacturing jobs by 2030 was published in 2021. It's 2026. We're more than halfway to the deadline and the gap has gotten worse, not better. The BLS outlook is negative. The training pipeline is inadequate. The wage dynamics are stuck in a structural trap.

The reshoring consensus — the one area of genuine bipartisan agreement in an era of political paralysis — depends on something that physically cannot happen without a force multiplier. You cannot reshore manufacturing to a country that is losing machinists. You cannot rebuild a defense industrial base that can't surge because it can't staff. You cannot spend $602 billion on AI infrastructure without dramatically increasing demand for the precision machining that infrastructure requires.

AI-powered manufacturing is not a nice-to-have. It is not a "digital transformation initiative" for some future strategic planning cycle. It is the load-bearing wall of every major economic and security initiative America is currently pursuing.

And the workforce that holds it all together is retiring.

The Choice

There are 354,800 people standing between the United States and industrial irrelevance. They are overwhelmingly male, disproportionately over 50, skilled beyond what most Americans can imagine, and they are not being replaced.

The conventional response to this — more training programs, higher wages, better marketing of manufacturing careers — is correct but insufficient. All of those things should happen. We should pay machinists more. We should fix apprenticeship programs that still teach G-code with pencils when they should be teaching AI-assisted CAM from day one. We should tell young people that CNC machining is one of the best career choices they could make: intellectually demanding, physically engaging, increasingly enhanced by technology, and desperately needed.

But the timeline doesn't bend. The demographic curve doesn't negotiate. The 2.1 million job gap by 2030 is not going to be closed by training programs that take years to produce graduates, for an occupation that takes years more to master. The math simply doesn't work.

What does work is making each machinist dramatically more productive. Not by working them harder — they're already working hard. Not by cutting corners on quality — the tolerances exist for a reason. By giving them tools that handle the repetitive, time-consuming, cognitively mundane portions of their work, so they can apply their irreplaceable expertise to more parts, more processes, more challenges.

A machinist who programs one part a day becomes a machinist who programs ten. A shop that turns away work becomes a shop that takes on everything that comes through the door. A defense contractor that can't find enough machinists discovers that the machinists it has can now cover the gap.

This is the last American advantage that matters: not that we have the most machinists — we don't. Not that we have the most machines — China leads there. Not that we have the cheapest labor — we never will. The advantage is that we are building the AI that turns each of our machinists into a one-person army, and that we are doing it while the generation that carries the knowledge is still here to encode it.

But "while they're still here" has an expiration date. The largest cohort of American machinists turns 60 this decade. The clock is running. The knowledge is perishable. The window is real.

Every year we delay deploying AI-powered manufacturing tools at scale is a year of irreplaceable expertise that walks out the door and doesn't come back. Every factory that waits for the "next version" or the "proven solution" is a factory whose best programmer is one retirement dinner away from crisis.

The money is flowing. The technology exists. The political will is there. The only question is whether we move fast enough.

For America's industrial base, for its defense capacity, for the reshoring dream that both parties say they believe in — fast enough means now. Not next quarter. Not after the next strategic review. Now.

The machinists aren't waiting for us to figure this out. They're just getting older.
